### 5.2 Abstract (≤150 words, unstructured EID format)

> In this observational ecological longitudinal study, Andes-virus hantavirus cardiopulmonary syndrome (HCPS) in Chile's Ñuble region has an incidence of 1.21/100,000 person-years (136 cases, 2002–2024), approximately 3–5× the national rate. We developed a three-tier early warning system coupling a walk-forward–validated negative-binomial mixed model with a satellite-derived forest-stress index (lag 5 months) and ERA5 temperature anomaly, pre-specified at Zenodo (DOI 10.5281/zenodo.19425753). Evaluated under the logarithmic score (Fox et al. 2024 EID), the model achieved 0.086 [95% CI 0.074, 0.098] at Tier 1 (surveillance) and 0.043 [0.022, 0.065] at Tier 2 (cluster alert). Brier Skill Scores against the PAHO endemic-channel baseline were 68.1% (95% CI 61.7–74.0) at Tier 1 and 36.5% (95% CI 25.4–56.6) at Tier 2. Tier 3 (outbreak, n = 20) is exploratory. External validation in other Andes-virus–endemic regions is essential before deployment.

### 5.4 Introduction (415 words)

Andes-virus hantavirus cardiopulmonary syndrome (HCPS) is a rodent-borne zoonosis endemic to southern South America, classified among neglected tropical diseases linked to poverty and ecosystem disturbance. It is transmitted by Oligoryzomys longicaudatus (long-tailed colilargo) and characterized by high case-fatality (≈28% in the Chilean Ñuble region, 2002–2024). Ñuble, in the south-central Andes foothills, sustained an incidence of 1.21 per 100,000 person-years during 2002–2024 — approximately 3–5× the national rate (Boletín MINSAL SE52 2024; Ortiz et al. 2004) — ranking among the top two regions nationally.

A central ecological hypothesis for this sustained endemicity is that sectoral synchronous flowering (not the classical mast event of >40°S C. culeou bamboo) of Chusquea quila triggers a subliminal two-to-fivefold rise in O. longicaudatus density — a phenomenon we term "ratización" to distinguish it from full ratadas (González 2001; Polop et al. 2010; Jaksic & Lima 2003; de la Fuente & Pacheco 2017) — which then supports persistent rather than epidemic viral circulation. Distinguishing this diffuse, sectoral mechanism from the Epuyén-type point-source cluster (Martínez et al. 2020 NEJM) is essential for the design of operational alert systems.

Probabilistic early warning systems for rodent- and vector-borne diseases have advanced rapidly through satellite remote sensing, reanalysis climate products, and forecast-verification methodology (Lowe et al. 2016; Colón-González et al. 2021; Reich et al. 2019). The Pan American Health Organization's endemic-channel method (Bortman 1999) remains the operational baseline in the Americas, but modern probabilistic models informed by remote-sensed covariates can substantially outperform it. Methodological rigour — strictly proper scoring rules, pre-registration, and explicit rare-event uncertainty handling — now separates publishable probabilistic EWS from those rejected for HARKing concerns.

We developed and validated a three-tier ecological EWS for Andes-virus HCPS in Ñuble built on a pre-registered generalized linear mixed model and a satellite-derived proxy. We evaluated forecast skill against three baselines of increasing stringency under a triple scoring rule framework (logarithmic score primary, ranked probability score complementary, scaled Brier with block-bootstrap confidence intervals as backward-comparable secondary) aligned with this journal's statistical consensus (Fox et al. 2024) and the forecast-verification literature (Gneiting & Raftery 2007; Wilks 2011), and characterized the FSI lag-5 operational lead time for intersectoral response. Tier 1 (surveillance: ≥1 case per comuna–month) and Tier 2 (cluster alert: sum ≥ 2 cases over 90 days) are the confirmatory alert levels. Tier 3 (outbreak declared: sum ≥ 3 cases over 180 days; n = 20 events) is reported throughout as an exploratory, hypothesis-generating alert level given small-sample constraints on discrimination metrics for rare outcomes (Minus et al. 2025).

### 5.5 Methods (991 words)

**5.5.1 Study design, setting and data sources**

We conducted an observational ecological longitudinal study using a comuna–month panel in Ñuble Region, south-central Chile (21 comunas, population ≈487,866), 2002–2024. The eco-epidemiological panel (5,796 comuna–month observations) contained: (i) monthly confirmed HCPS cases (n = 136; case definition: IgM seroconversion or RT-PCR positive per MINSAL circular B51/24) from the Ñuble SEREMI Salud Transparency Office and geolocation records. Ñuble was administratively established as an independent region by Law 21,033 of 5 September 2017, effective 6 September 2018; cases reported before that date (n = 103) were originally registered under the SEREMI Biobío regional office and were assigned to their probable commune of infection—rather than residence—**following the case reconciliation protocol described in Supplementary Methods**; (ii) ERA5-Land climate reanalysis (2-m temperature, precipitation) at ~11 km resolution; (iii) a custom Forest Stress Index (FSI) derived from Landsat 5/7/8 and Sentinel-2 NDMI/NDVI/NBR2 bands via Google Earth Engine, with active-burn pixels vetoed (ΔNBR2 < −0.15) to prevent wildfire-scar contamination (Ecke et al. 2019), calibrated in Achibueno and frozen before migration to Ñuble (anti-circularity); (iv) INE population estimates with log-linear intercensal interpolation; and (v) static covariates (forest cover fraction, elevation, slope). The panel dataset has SHA256 hash `0b87c5b4...802a`.

**5.5.2 Model specification (S29-K, locked in pre-specification v1.0)**

```
cases_it ~ NegBin(μ_it, θ)
log(μ_it) = β_0 + β_season · season_f_t + β_t2m · t2m_within_sc_it
          + ψ · R5_within_sc_it + β_pop · log(pop_it) + u_i
u_i ~ N(0, σ²_u)   (comuna random intercept, 21 clusters)
```

The negative-binomial family (Cameron & Trivedi 2005) accommodates rare-event overdispersion in zoonotic surveillance data. R5_within is the lag-5-month within-comuna forest stress index (biologically motivated by the 18–24 month C. quila→rodent→human lag; González 2001), t2m_within the within-comuna ERA5 2-m temperature anomaly, and season_f a 4-level categorical factor. All continuous predictors were Bell–Jones within–between decomposed (Bell & Jones 2015), with scaling after NA filtering of the lag structure (anti-leakage). The final specification was frozen before walk-forward evaluation (sessions S22–S29; see Supplementary Methods). Zone factor was dropped for redundancy with the comuna random intercept (LRT p = 0.186; Hodges & Reich 2010). With 136 events and 7 effective parameters, the event-to-parameter ratio is 19.4 (14.7 over 103 burn-in-excluded stable-fold events). The EPV ≥ 10 heuristic was derived for binary logistic regression and does not directly apply to negative-binomial counts; stability was confirmed by 94% bootstrap convergence (2,000 resamples) and consistent θ across stable folds (range 1.4–3.5).

**5.5.3 Walk-forward protocol with pre-specified burn-in period**

The walk-forward protocol comprised 14 expanding-window folds with test years 2011–2024; between- and within-comuna standardization parameters were recomputed using only each fold's training data. During the first four folds (2011–2014) the negative-binomial dispersion parameter θ exceeded 1×10⁶, converging to the Poisson limit — documented behaviour of the nbinom2 likelihood under sparse training counts (Brooks et al. 2017). Following Bergmeir et al. (2018) and Cerqueira et al. (2020), we pre-specified these four folds as a burn-in period (Addendum v1.1, lock date 2026-04-04) and report primary metrics over the ten stable folds (2015–2024, θ range 1.4–3.5); all metrics are re-computed over the full 14 folds in Supplementary Table S3.

**5.5.4 Three-tier alert definition**

- **Tier 1** (surveillance): ≥1 case per comuna–month
- **Tier 2** (cluster alert): rolling sum ≥ 2 cases over the prior 90 days
- **Tier 3** (outbreak declared, exploratory): rolling sum ≥ 3 cases over the prior 180 days

Predicted probabilities for each tier (p_ge1, p_ge2, p_ge3) are derived from the S29-K nbinom2 predictive distribution under the natural nesting relationship (Tier 3 ⇒ Tier 2 ⇒ Tier 1).

**5.5.5 Forecast evaluation (expanded per Addendum v1.1 and v1.2)**

**Primary**: logarithmic score (Good 1952; Gneiting & Raftery 2007; Fox et al. 2024 EID), strictly proper and free of denominator instability in the rare-event regime (Bosse et al. 2023). Uncertainty was quantified via block bootstrap (1,000 iterations, strata = walk-forward fold) with percentile 95% CI.

**Complementary**: ranked probability score on the nested ordinal outcome Y ∈ {0,1,2,3} (Epstein 1969; Murphy 1971), interval format per Bracher et al. 2021.

**Secondary**: scaled Brier score with bootstrap CI for backward comparability (Steyerberg et al. 2010). Formulas and scope notes are in Supplementary Methods.

**5.5.6 Multi-baseline skill assessment**

We evaluated skill against three baselines of increasing stringency:
1. **Bortman endemic channel** with +2 SD threshold (primary, PAHO/WHO standard; Bortman 1999)
2. **Poisson climatological null** including monthly seasonality and comuna random intercept (secondary stringent)
3. **Spatially and temporally invariant random forecast** at training-period prevalence (tertiary sanity floor)

Brier Skill Scores (BSS) are reported against each.

**5.5.7 Discrimination metrics**

We report ROC-AUC and precision-recall AUC (PR-AUC) with BCa bootstrap 95% CI from 2,000 row-level resamples (Efron 1987; Carpenter & Bithell 2000; Davison & Hinkley 1997; Saito & Rehmsmeier 2015; Sofaer et al. 2019); PR-AUC is particularly informative under severe class imbalance.

**5.5.8 Decision curve analysis**

We performed DCA (Vickers & Elkin 2006; Vickers et al. 2019; Assel et al. 2017) across threshold probabilities p_t ∈ [0, 0.25], comparing model net benefit to treat-all, treat-none, and the Bortman endemic-channel baseline on the net-benefit scale, annotating the operational range p_t ∈ [0.05, 0.20].

No prespecified subgroup analyses or fixed-effect interactions were planned; all inference is at the comuna–month panel level.

### 5.6 Results (1002 words)

**5.6.1 Epidemiological context**

During 2002–2024, 136 confirmed HCPS cases were registered in Ñuble (1.21/100k person-years), 91.2% occurring October–May and zero in September across 23 consecutive years. The panel was complete for climate, population, and geographic covariates; FSI lag-5 was structurally unavailable for the first five months of each commune's series (105 observations, 1.8%, excluded). Case-fatality was 27.9% (38/136; Wilson 95% CI 21.1–36.0%). Top-risk comunas were El Carmen (7.49/100k), Pinto (3.93), Coihueco (2.54), San Ignacio (2.36), and Yungay (2.08); Ninhue registered zero cases (structural zero). Of 5,796 comuna–month observations, 97.7% were zero-case, with a maximum of three months reaching a count of 3.

**5.6.2 Model coefficients and ecological interpretation (GLMM S29-K)**

The locked model yielded ψ_R5_within = −0.309 (95% profile CI [−0.546, −0.081], p = 0.009; IRR = 0.734 [0.580, 0.923]), β_t2m_within = +0.384 (p = 0.041; IRR = 1.468 [1.017, 2.130]), β_log_pop = +0.614 (p = 0.002), and nbinom2 θ = 1.555. The negative ψ reflects R as a post-flowering scar signal: high R means the C. quila cycle has already peaked and rodent density is regressing, so the operational risk window is low-R + warm conditions (active flowering phase). Within–between decomposition (Bell & Jones 2015) ruled out spatial confounding (ψ_between = −0.018, p = 0.935; Hausman p = 0.291).

**5.6.3 Headline forecast skill — primary logarithmic score (Table 1)**

The model achieved logarithmic scores of 0.086 [95% CI 0.074, 0.098] at Tier 1 (surveillance), 0.043 [0.022, 0.065] at Tier 2 (cluster alert), and 0.055 [0.000, 0.116] at Tier 3 (outbreak declared, exploratory; n = 20 events) in the primary 10-fold analysis (test period 2015–2024), comparable to ranges reported in this journal (Fox et al. 2024) and foundational scoring rule theory (Gneiting & Raftery 2007; Held et al. 2017).

Against the Bortman/PAHO endemic-channel baseline, Brier Skill Scores were 68.1% (95% CI 61.7–74.0) at Tier 1 and 36.5% (95% CI 25.4–56.6) at Tier 2 (block bootstrap, B=2000), exceeding dengue EWS values from Brazil and Vietnam (Lowe et al. 2016; Colón-González et al. 2021). The ranked probability score (three-tier nested ordinal) was 0.038 [95% CI 0.023, 0.053] versus a random-prevalence null of 0.038 — a +1.3% skill gain whose CI overlaps the null, directionally consistent but not statistically distinguishable at this sample size. Confirmatory claims therefore rest on the Tier 1 and Tier 2 BSS, which substantially exceed their reference distributions.

**5.6.5 Scoring rule stability**

Log score deltas between 10- and 14-fold analyses were Tier 1: −0.007, Tier 2: −0.014, Tier 3: −0.031, all within pre-specified robustness thresholds; bootstrap 95% CIs mutually contained each other's point estimates at all tiers. Scaled Brier showed +1,327% Tier-3 instability — a structural artifact of BS_max → 0 in the rare-event regime (Ferro & Fricker 2012) — confirming log score as the more appropriate primary metric (Supplementary Table S3).

**5.6.6 Decision curve analysis**

Decision curve analysis (Vickers & Elkin 2006) showed that Tier-1 and Tier-2 models provided positive net benefit across p_t ∈ [0.05, 0.20], while the Bortman baseline approached zero or turned negative for p_t > 0.05. At p_t = 0.05 Tier-1 net benefit was approximately 3× Bortman. Treat-all and treat-none were dominated by the model beyond the base-rate prevalence.

### 5.7 Discussion (952 words)

Our pre-registered three-tier EWS achieves substantial skill against the PAHO endemic-channel baseline (Tables 1–2; Tier 1 and Tier 2 CIs exclude zero), comparable to leading dengue EWS from Brazil and Vietnam (Lowe et al. 2016; Colón-González et al. 2021). Against a stringent Poisson null absorbing seasonality and spatial heterogeneity, incremental BSS is modest (1.97% Tier 1, 1.49% Tier 2) — consistent with the CDC FluSight ensemble, where adding models to a strong baseline yielded ≈2% gains with reduced variability (Fox et al. 2024 EID). The residual skill reflects the net contribution of ERA5 temperature anomaly and FSI lag 5 beyond seasonality and space.

Recent hantavirus EWS include Kazasidis et al. (2024 Sci Rep), who reached 82.8% accuracy for Puumala virus in Germany using SVM on weather and beech flowering, and Ferro et al. (2024 Pathogens), who used rodent abundance with 3–8 month climate lags for Andes virus in northwestern Argentina. Analogous scoring-rule principles have guided probabilistic evaluation in other zoonotic settings (Funk et al. 2019, Ebola in Sierra Leone). Our satellite-only approach with a single pre-specified FSI proxy is complementary: no field trapping, commune-level output, 5-month lead time.

Tier 3 remains exploratory pending external validation; under the log score primary, 10- vs 14-fold differences fall within sampling noise (mutual CI overlap), supporting Tier 1–2 robustness.

**Ecological framing**. The negative ψ (IRR = 0.734 per 1 SD of R5_within) is biologically coherent with C. quila sectoral flowering as a diffuse phenomenon: high R marks a post-flowering satellite scar, meaning the cycle has peaked and the Oligoryzomys population is regressing toward baseline. The operational risk window is low-R combined with warm conditions. This is biologically distinct from the Epuyén-type point-source cluster (Martínez et al. 2020 NEJM), where person-to-person Andes virus transmission dominates. Our model targets sectoral endemic persistence, not person-to-person amplification, and should not be extrapolated without redesign.

**Public health implications**. The Bortman/PAHO endemic channel detects threshold exceedance from already-reported cases — retrospective surveillance without advance warning. Our FSI lag-5 model generates risk predictions 5 months before expected case onset, during the C. quila confinement phase, enabling action before the seasonal peak. This prospective lead time is the main operational advance.

The three-tier framework maps onto operational response windows for Chile's intersectoral hantavirus coordination mechanism (MINSAL Ordinario B38 N°3420, 2019). At FSI detection (t = 0), CONAF restricts access to identified quiloide patches; during the 5-month confinement phase, SEREMI Salud deploys peridomestic education in high-risk communes; at the dispersal threshold (t + 5), SAG inspects rural storage facilities in ecotone areas. To our knowledge, this biphasic protocol — confinement followed by dispersal — is the first satellite-triggered multi-agency hantavirus early warning specifying temporal action windows from a single pre-specified environmental proxy. CONAF patrol coverage is seasonal (October–April), only partially overlapping the primary risk window; year-round monitoring requires SEREMI coordination with municipal authorities.

**Companion study disclosure**. A companion clinical analysis of 34 HCPS patients treated at Hospital Clínico Herminda Martín, Chillán, 2010–2025 has been submitted separately (Contreras et al., Revista Chilena de Infectología). It addresses clinical characterization, severity, and an early prognostic triad (respiratory rate >22/min, platelets <150,000/μL, hematocrit above ULN) using Firth-penalized logistic regression. The present ecological and companion clinical studies share no analytical methods, tables, figures, or computed metrics; only publicly available regional descriptors (total case count, case-fatality ratio, ethics approval) are common. Both manuscripts were designed as complementary analyses under ICMJE criteria for acceptable secondary publication, with full disclosure to both journals.

**Limitations (8 declared)**:
1. **Modest incremental skill vs stringent null** (~2% Tier-1 BSS residual over Poisson null — expected per Fox 2024)
2. **Burn-in period** (4 folds 2011-2014, θ>10⁶ Poisson limit; Brooks 2017 documented, not failure)
3. **Tier 3 exploratory designation** (20 events → wide CIs; exploratory under all scoring formulations)
4. **Scoring rule choice** (log score primary per Gneiting-Raftery; scaled Brier retained for comparability)
5. **Ecological fallacy** (comuna-month unit; Robinson 1950; Morgenstern 1995)
6. **Specification search** (S22-S29 alternatives documented; frozen pre-walk-forward; E-values = 2.07/2.30)
7. **External validation** (confined to Ñuble; southern Argentina transferability needs empirical verification)
8. **Exposure misclassification** (commune of probable infection; 87% peridomestic per Riquelme 2015; biases toward null per Yland 2022; Hoek 2024)

### 5.8 Conclusions (109 words)

> We report a pre-registered three-tier probabilistic EWS for Andes-virus HCPS in Ñuble, Chile, evaluated under a triple scoring rule framework aligned with current Emerging Infectious Diseases standards. Tier-1 and Tier-2 Brier Skill Scores against the PAHO endemic-channel baseline are 68.1% (95% CI 61.7–74.0) and 36.5% (95% CI 25.4–56.6), outperforming the current standard, with pre-specification, walk-forward validation, proper scoring rules, and Zenodo archiving. Tier 3 is explicitly exploratory. Against the regional backdrop of the **PAHO December 2025 epidemiological alert documenting renewed hantavirus activity across the Americas (PAHO 2025)**, the work supports adoption of modern probabilistic verification in national surveillance and provides a reproducible template for rare-zoonosis EWS.
